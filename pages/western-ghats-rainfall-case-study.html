Study Area

The Western Ghats — A Climate-Sensitive Orographic System

Stretching approximately 1,600 km along India's western coastline, the Western Ghats constitute one of the world's most ecologically significant mountain systems and a UNESCO World Heritage region. Acting as a dominant orographic barrier, the range intercepts moisture-laden Arabian Sea winds, driving monsoon precipitation gradients across peninsular India.

The region sustains major river basins — including the Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri — and supports extraordinary biodiversity. Increasing variability in extreme rainfall, landslides, and hydrological instability makes regionally segmented climate projection analysis both scientifically and socio-economically critical.

Data Architecture

CMIP6 Scenario Framework

This analysis utilises precipitation projections from the CMIP6 EC-Earth3 Global Climate Model under the SSP2-4.5 stabilisation pathway, spanning 2025–2100. Data were processed into annual aggregates to enable long-term trend assessment across sub-regions.

EC-Earth3 demonstrates strong performance in simulating Indian monsoon dynamics and orographic precipitation, making it suitable for focused regional analysis.

Regional Segmentation

Methods

Statistical & Analytical Framework

Projected precipitation was aggregated annually per segment and evaluated across three climate windows:

Near-century

2025–2050

Mid-century

2051–2075

Late-century

2076–2100

Mann–Kendall Test

Non-parametric monotonic trend detection — no distributional assumptions required, suitable for skewed hydro-climatic time series.

Sen's Slope Estimator

Median-based rate-of-change measure (mm/year), robust to outliers — quantifies the magnitude of detected rainfall trends.

Implications

Emerging Signals & Regional Implications

Spatially heterogeneous rainfall intensification patterns detected across Western Ghats under SSP2-4.5.

Southern zones demonstrate amplified variability and stronger late-century intensification signals.

Mid-century transitional behaviour identified in Central Ghats — potential hydrological regime shift.

Findings underscore the need for sub-regional climate analytics to support adaptive planning and ecosystem resilience strategies.
