What is COP?

Once a Year, the World Sits Down to Talk Climate

Every autumn, nearly every nation on Earth — from climate-vulnerable small island states to the world's largest fossil fuel producers — sends its ministers, scientists, and diplomats to a single city to do something uniquely difficult: agree on how to address climate change together. This gathering is called the Conference of the Parties, or COP.

Think of it as the world's largest climate board meeting, one where agenda items were set three decades ago in Rio de Janeiro and where every formal decision requires consensus. That structural tension — between urgency and unanimity — explains both the ambition and the frustration that follow every COP.

The "Parties" are the countries that have signed one or more of the major international climate treaties. COP is the annual session of the governing body of those treaties. COP30 was the 30th such session, held in Belém, Brazil — the gateway city to the Amazon rainforest — from 10 to 22 November 2025. Brazil's choice of venue was deliberate: to seat the world's climate negotiations inside one of its most vital carbon sinks.

"COP30 was the moment the world was asked to stop making promises and start delivering them — a COP not of ambition, but of implementation."

— Brazilian COP30 Presidency

Brazil branded it the "COP of Implementation." After years of landmark declarations — the Paris Agreement (2015), the Glasgow Climate Pact (2021), the UAE Consensus (2023) — the central question in Belém was stark: who is actually doing what they said they would?

The Treaties
The Legal Framework

Three Treaties That Govern the World's Climate

To understand what happens at COP, you need to understand the legal architecture beneath it. Three agreements form the backbone of international climate law — each one building on, and in some ways correcting, the last.

Adopted 1992 · Rio de Janeiro

UNFCCC — The Parent Treaty

The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change is the founding document of global climate governance. It established the principle that countries must cooperate to avoid "dangerous" interference with the climate system. Crucially, it introduced the idea of "common but differentiated responsibilities" — wealthy nations that industrialised first bear greater obligations. All 197 countries in the world are parties, making it near-universal. The COP is literally the annual meeting of its signatories.

Adopted 1997 · Kyoto, Japan

Kyoto Protocol — The First Hard Targets

The first treaty to impose legally binding emission reduction targets — but only on wealthy, developed nations (called "Annex I" countries). Developing nations, including China and India, had no binding targets, which became a major political fault line. The US signed but never ratified. Canada withdrew in 2011. Its second commitment period ran until 2020, after which it was effectively superseded by Paris. Today, 192 countries remain parties, though it is largely historical.

Adopted 2015 · Paris, France

Paris Agreement — The Living Framework

The centrepiece of modern climate diplomacy. Its breakthrough was universal participation: every country — rich or poor — sets its own climate targets, called Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). The shared ceiling: limit warming to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, with efforts toward 1.5°C. 195 parties have ratified it. The US rejoined under Biden (2021) but withdrew again under Trump in early 2025 — making its absence at COP30 the first in COP history.

Annual meeting · Berlin 1995 → present

COP — The Decision-Making Body

The COP is not itself a treaty — it is the supreme decision-making body of the UNFCCC that also serves the Paris Agreement and Kyoto Protocol. It reviews implementation, adopts new decisions, and keeps the entire process alive year to year. Decisions require consensus, which is why a handful of resistant states can — and regularly do — water down final text. All 197 UNFCCC members are COP members.

UNFCCC · 197 parties Paris Agreement · 195 parties Kyoto Protocol · 192 parties

At COP30, the operative work centred almost entirely on the Paris Agreement: were countries delivering on their NDCs, were richer nations funding adaptation in the developing world, and could the 1.5°C limit still be kept alive?

Mission & Objectives
What COP30 Set Out to Do

Three Mandates for Belém

Every COP has a presidency — the host country that sets the agenda, facilitates negotiations, and is judged by what its summit produces. Brazil's COP30 presidency, led by veteran climate diplomat André Corrêa do Lago, framed three core objectives around which every negotiation in Belém was organised.

I

Strengthen multilateralism

In a geopolitically fractured 2025 — the US absent, geopolitical rivalries intensifying — Brazil sought to prove that international climate cooperation could still function. The concept of mutirão, a Brazilian cultural tradition of collective community effort, became the presidency's watchword and eventually lent its name to the final decision text.

II

Connect climate to people

Abstract targets mean little without visible links to livelihoods, health, and justice. COP30 explicitly centred Indigenous Peoples (over 5,000 participants — a COP record), women, Afro-descendant communities, and subnational governments. The recognition that "implementation happens locally" shaped both the formal text and the Action Agenda running alongside it.

III

Accelerate Paris delivery

Countries were asked to submit stronger third-generation NDCs (NDCs 3.0), close the yawning gap between pledges and science, and mobilise climate finance — especially for the developing world. As UN Secretary-General António Guterres said at COP30's opening: "The 1.5°C limit is a red line for humanity. It must be kept within reach."

Events at COP30
A Summit Within a Summit

The Many Worlds Inside COP30

COP is not one meeting. It is dozens of simultaneous processes: formal treaty negotiations in closed rooms, ministerial-level roundtables, civil society summits, initiative launches, and bilateral deals struck in corridors. Here is a map of what unfolded across Belém's 13 days.

01

Leaders' Summit

Heads of government from the UK, France, Germany, and Brazil met before formal talks opened. India, the US, and China did not attend at head-of-state level — a signal of geopolitical strain.

02

Formal UNFCCC Negotiations

Closed-door talks between ~197 national delegations, running in parallel streams on mitigation, adaptation, finance, technology transfer, and the global stocktake follow-up.

03

COP30 Action Agenda

A parallel voluntary track of commitments by businesses, cities, and NGOs — designed to translate formal pledges into on-the-ground implementation between COPs.

04

People's Summit (Cúpula dos Povos)

A major civil society counter-summit on climate justice and Indigenous rights, running alongside the official conference. Mid-week, Indigenous groups staged blockades demanding stronger Amazon protections.

05

Tropical Forests Forever Fund

$5.5 billion raised. 53 countries joined. At least 20% of resources flow directly to Indigenous Peoples and local communities — a landmark shift in forest finance architecture.

06

Belém Health Action Plan

The first global initiative explicitly targeting climate-related health threats, launched with $300 million from 35 philanthropic organisations across 35 countries.

07

Carbon Markets Coalition

An Open Coalition on Compliance Carbon Markets was formed, with Brazil, China, EU, UK, Canada, Chile, Germany, Mexico, and others committing to improve carbon pricing mechanisms.

08

Urban-Climate Ministerial

Cities and subnational governments secured formal recognition of their role in Paris Agreement delivery via mechanisms like CHAMP — a breakthrough for multilevel governance advocates.

09

Finance & Ministerial Dialogues

High-level talks on mobilising the tripled adaptation finance pledged in Baku (COP29), operationalising the Loss and Damage Fund, and aligning global trade flows with climate objectives.

10

Gender Action Plan Adoption

The Belém Gender Action Plan was formally adopted — embedding gender equality across climate finance, adaptation planning, and formal decision-making processes through 2030.

11

Daring Cities 2025

A global town hall for mayors and local leaders to align city-level climate action with the COP30 Action Agenda — bringing subnational voices into the formal presidency narrative.

12

Disinformation on the Agenda

For the first time in COP history, climate misinformation was a formal agenda item. Parties committed to promoting information integrity — a quiet but significant milestone.

A fire broke out at the venue on the penultimate night, disrupting a critical stretch of talks. Negotiators worked through to the early hours of Saturday, 22 November — a day past schedule — to produce a final text.

Key Decisions
What Was Decided

The Belém Political Package: Wins, Gaps and Open Questions

COP30's formal output was a bundle of decisions called the "Belém Political Package." The headline document — the Mutirão Decision — called on humanity to unite in collective effort and formally transitioned the climate regime from a negotiation phase into an implementation phase. Here is what it contained, and what it left unresolved.

Issue What was decided Status
Climate Finance
Tripling of adaptation finance for developing nations by 2035. A Just Transition Mechanism (JTM) established to support workers and communities in the shift away from fossil fuels.
Win
Loss & Damage Fund
The fund for countries suffering irreversible climate impacts (agreed at COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh) had its operational structure and replenishment cycles formally confirmed in Belém.
Win
Global Implementation Accelerator
A new body to fast-track delivery of climate pledges, with priority areas including methane reduction and nature-based carbon removal. Will report findings at COP31 in 2026.
Win
Belém Mission to 1.5°C
First-ever COP decision to explicitly acknowledge the possibility of overshooting the 1.5°C threshold and the need to limit its magnitude and duration. A significant rhetorical and legal shift.
Win
Adaptation Indicators
59 global indicators for measuring adaptation progress were formally adopted — a technical cornerstone that enables systematic tracking of how well nations are protecting their populations.
Win
Fossil Fuel Phase-Out
More than 80 countries backed formal roadmap language. Petrostates blocked it. The COP30 president announced two voluntary roadmaps — on fossil fuels and forests — outside formal UN processes. No binding language was adopted.
Partial
Deforestation Roadmap
Despite COP30's Amazon location, a binding global deforestation roadmap was not agreed. A voluntary presidency roadmap was announced, but forest protection commitments remain fragmented internationally.
Gap
US Absence
For the first time in COP history, the US government sent no official delegation. California Governor Gavin Newsom attended independently to promote state-level commitments and signal continued US subnational engagement.
Concern

The science context is sobering: current NDCs from all parties combined would reduce emissions to roughly 12% below 2019 levels by 2035. Scientists say the world needs a 43% reduction on that timeline to stay on a 1.5°C pathway. The gap between pledge and trajectory remains, in the words of the UN Secretary-General, "dangerously wide."

— Looking Ahead

COP31 and the Road to Antalya

The next conference — COP31 — will be hosted by Turkey in Antalya, after Australia agreed to step back from a competing bid in exchange for chairing the talks and hosting a pre-COP in the Pacific region.

Key milestones before COP31:

April 2026 — Fossil fuel transition consultations co-hosted by the COP30 Presidency begin. June and November 2026 — The Global Implementation Accelerator and Belém Mission to 1.5°C hold open sessions. Countries that did not submit credible NDCs face mounting pressure to deliver updated plans well ahead of Antalya.

Final Word

Why COP Matters — Even When It Disappoints

Climate negotiations move slowly — frustratingly so. Every COP produces a gap between what scientists demand and what diplomats can agree. But COP also represents something genuinely rare in global governance: a space where the world's most climate-vulnerable nations — low-lying Pacific islands, drought-stricken Sahel countries, flood-prone coastal communities — have a formal seat alongside the world's largest emitters.

Belém did not solve the climate crisis. No single COP can. But it held the multilateral framework together at a moment when it seemed genuinely at risk of fracturing; it produced concrete commitments on adaptation finance and institutional architecture; and it maintained the legal and political scaffolding through which future ambition can be built. For those working at the intersection of climate science, resilience planning, and public policy — the work of COPs is never finished, only continued.
